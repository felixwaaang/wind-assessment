Preliminary Wind Resource Assessment

1.1 DataSource

There were no suitable measurements obtained on-site. The primary source of wind data used to characterize the wind climate prevailing at the site was the ERA5 reanalysis dataset. Atmospheric reanalysis is a hindcast process that assimilates data from multiple sources of observation into a simulation to provide a single consistent representation of past meteorological conditions. ERA5 (ECMWF Re-Analysis 5) is a reanalysis data product provided by European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). Alternative sources of reanalysis data include MERRA-2. ERA5 was selected because the node for which it was available was closest to the site, and studies comparing reanalysis and direct observation in this region indicate ERA5 performs most satisfactorily.

Four close ERA5 data nodes are used to interpolate the condition at the potential site of the wind farm (28.25, 34.9). The four nodes are located at: (28.25, 34.75) and (28.25, 35.0)respectively, as shown in Figure 1.1. Hourly wind speed, direction, temperature, and pressure values at a height of 100 meters were obtained for a PoR of 6 years from 2019-01-01 00:00:00 to 2024-08-17 17:00:00. This PoR was considered sufficiently representative of long-term conditions on-site, and 100 meters was considered the height closest to potential wind turbine generator (WTG) hub heights for which data were available.

These wind data are suitable for the purposes of preliminary energy yield estimation for conceptual design, feasibility studies, WTG technology selection, and project configuration; however, site classification, engineering design, and bank-grade energy yield estimation for financial purposes require direct wind measurements obtained on-site for a PoR of at least 1 year to support confidence in the observed wind climate and provide detailed information regarding directly measured wind conditions. These measurements may be made using light detection and ranging (LiDAR) in a variety of configurations (e.g., vertically profiling or scanning instruments installed on the platforms, or floating LiDAR systems deployed near to the platforms). Black & Veatch has in-depth experience and expertise with these measurement technologies and stand ready to advise should it be deemed necessary to design and execute a wind measurement campaign.

1.2 Wind Analysis

1.2.1 Weibull Distribution

Wind speed data were density corrected to 1.225 kg/m3 as this was the air density for which WTG power curve information was valid. The density correction was applied using temperature and pressure data in accordance with IEC 61400-12-1:2005, Clause 8.1. The average density observed in the data was 1.162 kg/m3. The mean wind speed at 100 meters recorded in the ERA5 data during the PoR was 5.803 m/s while the mean density corrected value was 5.7 m/s. All the wind speeds are mean wind spend in this study.

The wind data were extrapolated from 100 meters to a height of 150 meters, which was considered to be more representative of the eventual WTG hub height. A power law model of wind shear with an exponent of 0.1 was applied to extrapolate wind speeds to a hub height of 150 meters above sea level. The mean wind speed during the PoR after density correction and extrapolation to a hub height of 150 meters was 5.936 m/s. The hub height of 150 meters includes the height of the tower and the height interval between the interface of the tower with the foundation at the transition piece and the mean sea level. Heights are stated relative to sea level. For example, the height of the tower may be approximately 130 meters and the interval between the interface of the tower with the foundation and sea level may be approximately 20 meters. A wind shear exponent, α, of 0.1 was considered representative of offshore conditions. The wind shear exponent describes how wind speed varies with height in accordance with a power law: u1/u0 = (z1/z0) α where u1 is the wind speed at height z1, u0 is the wind speed at height z0, and z1 and z0 are heights 1 and 0. α is the wind shear exponent, which is 0.1 in this case. A wind measurement campaign on-site would be required to establish this more precisely. The wind shear is determined by influences such as the wave climate and the prevailing atmospheric stability and stratification, which are likely to exhibit strong directional, diurnal, and seasonal dependencies.

1.2.2 Directional and Seasonal Variations

The directional variation is shown in the wind rose on Figure 1.3. There are 2 main directions represented in the wind rose, including average winds: winds from North-Northeast with average wind speed of 4.36 m/s, winds from North with average wind speed of 6.66 m/s.

The seasonal variation is shown on Figure 1.4, where the 8,760 hours of the year are represented by the angle of a polar plot and the average wind speed for that hour by the radius. The year proceeds clockwise from the January. A circle is fitted to the data, and the displacement of the center of the circle from the origin corresponds to the high season when the strongest winds occur. This is in June, with a seasonality of 20.8 percent, which is the ratio of the distance of the center of the circle from the origin to the average wind speed, normalized by a factor of π/4 corresponding to the maximum value of the average that occurs for a unit circle passing through the origin.

The seasonal variation is also represented by the monthly averages shown on Figure 1.5 and Figure 1.6. The periods in June and September have much higher wind speed. The monthly wind rose illustrates dramatic wind orientation in different months on Figure 1.6.
